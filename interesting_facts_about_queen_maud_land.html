Interesting facts about Queen Maud Land

Interesting facts about Queen Maud Land presented here are related to the most diverse topics of both the modern life and the past of the territory, including (but not limited to) its history, heritage, nature, culture, science and of course people. Queen Maud Land is a vast area on the Atlantic coast of Antarctica, which Norway claims as its dependent territory. The area is about 2,500,000 sq km. It's famous for the least known species of Antarctic seals, a glacial blue ice runway, shark cave mountain and not only. So get ready to explore some interesting and fun facts about Queen Maud Land.

Fact #1. Queen Maud Land is a Norwegian part of the continent of Antarctica

The first one of fun facts about Queen Maud Land regards Norway. Queen Maud Land is a 2,7 million sq km (1,04 million sq miles) region of Antarctica, lying between 20 degrees west and 45 degrees east, claimed as a dependent territory by Norway.

Fact #2. Queen Maud Land is named after the Norwegian queen

Queen Maud Land is named after the Norwegian queen Maud of Wales. Maud Charlotte Mary Victoria (26 November 1869 - 20 November 1938) was Queen of Norway and the youngest daughter of King Edward VII and Queen Alexandra of the U.K.

Fact #3. Queen Maud Land was discovered by aviation pioneer

Here's another one of interesting facts about Queen Maud Land. The first man to set foot on this territory was Norwegian aviation pioneer and polar explorer Hjalmar Riiser-Larsen. He made it in 1930. In 1939, the territory was claimed by Norway.

Fact #4. Queen Maud Land is not the only Norwegian Antarctic claim

Queen Maud Land is one of two Antarctic claims ever made by Norway. The other one is Peter I Island. Both claims are administered by the Polar Affairs Department of the Norwegian Ministry of Justice and Public Security located in Oslo.

Fact #5. Queen Maud Land is home to the least well known species of Antarctic seals

Here goes the next one of fun facts about Queen Maud Land. The territory is washed by the King Haakon VII Sea which is the natural habitat for Ross seals. These are the smallest, least abundant and least well known species of seals of Antarctica.

Fact #6. There is a shark cave mountain in Queen Maud Land

Hahellerskarvet is a 2,910 m (9,550 ft) high partially ice-covered mountain, located between Austreskorve Glacier and Lunde Glacier in the Muhlig-Hoffman Mountains of Queen Maud Land. Hahellerskarvet means "shark cave mountain" in Norwegian.

Fact #7. There are 12 active research stations in Queen Maud Land

Here's another one of interesting facts about Queen Maud Land. This land is home to 12 active research stations, of which 6 are occupied year-round, while the remainder are seasonal summer stations, the total summer population of which is 494.

Fact #8. The most abundant species of birds in Queen Maud Land can run on water

The snow petrel is a 36 to 41 cm (14-16 in) long bird native to the Geographic South Pole. The word "petrel" is derived from Peter the Apostle and the story of his walking on water. This is the reference to the petrel's habit to run on the water to take off.

Fact #9. Norwegian King became the world's first reigning monarch to visit Antarctica

Here goes the next one of fun facts about Queen Maud Land. The summer station Troll was established here in 1989-90. In 2015, King Harald V became the first reigning monarch in the world to visit Antarctica when he went to Queen Maud Land.

Fact #10. There is a glacial blue ice runway in Queen Maud Land

The main aerodrome of Queen Maud Land is Troll Airfield. It's a 3,300 by 100 m (10,830 by 330 ft) runway on glacial blue ice on the Antarctic ice sheet. Intercontinental flights normally operate from Cape Town using Ilyushin 76, C-130 Hercules and P-3 Orion.
