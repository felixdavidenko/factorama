Interesting facts about Peter I Island

Interesting facts about Peter I Island presented here are related to the most diverse topics of both the modern life and the past of the island, including (but not limited to) its history, heritage, nature, culture, science and of course people. Peter I Island (Peter the First Island) is an uninhabited island in the Antarctic Pacific Ocean, in the Bellingshausen Sea, 400 km (248 mi) from the Antarctic coast. The island is famous for its ultra mountain, the world's most abundant bird species, the DX-pedition and not only. So get ready to explore some interesting and fun facts about Peter I Island.

Fact #1. Peter I Island is a Norwegian dependency in the Antarctic

Here goes the first one of fun facts about Peter I Island. It's an uninhabited volcanic island, located some 450 km (280 mi) off the continent of Antarctica. Peter I Island is one of the three Norwegian dependencies in the Antarctic and Subantarctic.

Fact #2. Peter I Island is a 0,35 million years old volcano

It's known that Peter I Island is a 0,1 to 0,35 million years old Holocene shield volcano, but it's still unknown if it's active or not. Since the upper part of the island is unmodified by glaciation, it means that it last erupted approximately several centuries ago.

Fact #3. Peter I Island has its own ultra

Here's another one of interesting facts about Peter I Island. An ultra is a mountain summit with a prominence of over 1,500 m (4,900 ft). The local ultra is a Lars Christensen Peak, which is a 1,640 m (5,381 ft) tall shield volcano off the coast of Antarctica.

Fact #4. Peter I Island is home to one of the world's most abundant bird species

Peter I Island is home to several species of seabirds, especially Wilson's storm petrels. Wilson's storm petrel is a small seabird named after the Scottish-American ornithologist Alexander Wilson. Its world population is estimated at 50 million individual species.

Fact #5. Most part of the year Peter I Island is inaccessible

The next one of fun facts about Peter I Island regards pack ice. Almost the entire island is covered with a glacier, most of the year surrounded by pack ice. Also known as drift ice or brash ice, it makes the island almost inaccessible for ships most of the year.

Fact #6. The only vegetation on Peter I Island are mosses and lichens

Peter I Island has a very harsh climate with strong winds and extremely low temperatures, making the island's vegetation very sparse and consisting of only lichens and mosses. Consistent snowfall and freezing temperatures keep vegetation at a minimum.

Fact #7. Peter I Island was discovered by Russian and named after the Russian Tsar

Here goes another one of interesting facts about Peter I Island. The island was first discovered on 21 October 1821 by Russian naval officer, cartographer and explorer Fabian Gottlieb Bellingshausen, who named it for Tsar Peter I the Great of Russia.

Fact #8. Peter I Island became Norwegian 112 years after it was discovered

Because of pack ice, several first expeditions didn't land on the island. The second Norvegia expedition made it on 2 February 1929 and immediately claimed the island for Norway. On 23 March 1933, the island was declared a Norwegian dependency.

Fact #9. Peter I Island was thrice the site of the DX-pedition

The next one of fun facts about Peter I Island regards radio. The island was a site of the three DX-peditions, in 1987, 1994 and 2006. A DX-pedition is an amateur expedition to some exotic place with a goal to make two-way radio contact with distant stations.

Fact #10. Peter I Island is home to the largest extant marine mammal

Peter I Island is densely populated with the southern elephant seals. The southern elephant seal is known as the largest extant marine mammal which is not a cetacean. The regular species weigh up to 900 kg (1,980 lb) and measure up to 3 m (9,8 ft) long.
