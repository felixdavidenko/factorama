Interesting facts about Argentine Antarctica

Interesting facts about Argentine Antarctica presented here are related to the most diverse topics of both the modern life and the past of this territory, including (but not limited to) its history, heritage, nature, culture, science, and of course people. Argentine Antarctica is part of Antarctica claimed by Argentina. It includes the Antarctic Peninsula and a triangular sector stretching from the waters of the Atlantic Ocean to the South Pole. The territory is famous for being a rocket launching site, an entrance door of Antarctica, a popular tourist destination, and not only. Here you will find amazing, funny and of course interesting facts about Argentine Antarctica for children, adults, the elderly, and in general everyone who is interested in the world that surrounds us and the facts that it is filled with. Enjoy reading.

Fact #1. Argentine Antarctica is claimed and ruled by Argentina

Here goes the first one of fun facts about Argentine Antarctica. It's a sector of Antarctica claimed by Argentina as part of its national territory. It consists of the Antarctic Peninsula and a triangular section extending straight to the center of the South Pole.

Fact #2. Argentine Antarctica was claimed by a secret mission

"Operacion 90" was the first Argentine ground expedition to the South Pole, conducted in 1965 and performed to attempt to cement Argentina's claims to a portion of Antarctica. It was kept in secret so as not to upset the superpowers of the time, the US and USSR.

Fact #3. The total population of Argentine Antarctica is 230

Here’s another one of interesting facts about Argentine Antarctica. According to the last census, there were 230 inhabitants at the 6 permanent stations: 75 at Marambio, 66 at Esperanza, 33 at Jubany, 20 at San Martín, 19 at Belgrano II, and 17 at Orcadas.

Fact #4. Argentine Antarctica is home to one of the largest Adelie penguin colonies in Antarctica

Hope Bay on Trinity Peninsula is an Important Bird Area (IBA) by BirdLife International because it supports one of the largest Adelie penguin colonies in Antarctica with around 125,000 pairs. It's also the site of the Argentinian Esperanza Base, established in 1952.

Fact #5. Argentine Antarctica once was a rocket launching site

The next one of fun facts about Argentine Antarctica regards rocketry. Between the mid-1960s and the 1970s, Argentina launched several rockets from its Antarctic bases. The rockets were built entirely in Argentina and possessed meteorological sensors.

Fact #6. The horseshoe-shaped volcanic caldera is a major tourist spot in Argentine Antarctica

Deception Island is the caldera of an active volcano, located in the South Shetland Islands. The island is roughly circular and horseshoe-shaped, with a max diameter of 15 km (9,3 mi). It's a popular tourist destination and scientific outpost with research stations.

Fact #7. There is an island in Argentine Antarctica named after the feast of the circumcision

Another one of interesting facts about Argentine Antarctica regards Petermann Island. There is a cove on its southeast side, named Port Circumcision. It was named so because it was spotted on January 1, 1909, the traditional day for the Feast of the Circumcision.

Fact #8. Antarctica's Entrance Door is located in Argentine Antarctica

Marambio Station is a permanent all-year-round station founded in 1969. At the time of its construction, it was the first airfield in Antarctica and is still one of the most frequently used. That's why this airfield is also called the "Antarctica's Entrance Door".

Fact #9. The first children born in Antarctica were born in Argentine Antarctica

Here’s the next one of fun facts about Argentine Antarctica. The first baby ever born in Antarctica was Emilio Marcos Palma. He was born on 7 January 1978. The first girl born on the Antarctic continent was Marisa Delgado, born on May 27, 1978.

Fact #10. Argentine Antarctica is home to the oldest existing Antarctic human settlement

On 2 January 1904 Argentina acquired the weather station installed by Scotsman William Speirs Bruce in Laurie Island. Over time this observatory became the oldest existing today across the entire Antarctic territory permanent human settlement.
