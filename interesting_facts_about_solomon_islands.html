Interesting facts about Solomon Islands

Interesting facts about Solomon Islands presented here are related to the most diverse topics of both the modern life and the past of the country, including (but not limited to) its history, heritage, nature, culture, science, and of course people. The Solomon Islands is a state in the South Pacific Ocean. The islands have preserved many memorable places from the Second World War. On Guadalcanal, one of the largest islands in the archipelago, there is an American war memorial to the fallen soldiers of the Allied forces. In Honiara, the capital of the country, the central market hosts exhibitions of locally made goods and products. The country is also famous for its world's largest coral atoll, shell money, submarine volcano and not only. Here you will find amazing, funny and of course interesting facts about Solomon Islands for children, adults, the elderly, and in general everyone who is interested in the world that surrounds us and the facts that it is filled with. Fact #1. The name of Solomon Islands was a result of mistaken assumption

Here goes the first one of fun facts about Solomon Islands. In 1568, the Spanish navigator Alvaro de Medana named islands "Solomon" in the name of the wealthy biblical King Solomon, because of the mistaken assumption that islands contain great riches.

Fact #2. The western and the eastern of Solomon Islands are located 1,500 km from each other

Solomon Islands is an island nation. Its islands are scattered in the Pacific Ocean. The distance between the westernmost and the easternmost islands is estimated at 1,500 km (930 mi). The Santa Cruz Islands are located 200 km (120 mi) from the other islands.

Fact #3. There is a market in Solomon Islands that determines the country's state

Here's another one of interesting facts about Solomon Islands. The Honiara Central Market, located in the country's capital is a major market that "dominates" the national trade. The market is so important that it's typified as an indicator of the country's state.

Fact #4. There is a province in Solomon Islands named after the Spanish village

Guadalcanal is a 5,336 sq km (2,510 sq mi) island mostly covered with jungle. It's also one of the nine provinces of Solomon Islands. This island was named so by Pedro de Ortega Valencia, who was born in the village of Guadalcanal, located in Seville, Spain.

Fact #5. The world's largest coral atoll is located in Solomon Islands

The next one of fun facts about Solomon Islands regards East Rennell island. Rennell is the southernmost of the Solomon Islands, the UNESCO World Heritage Site, and the world's largest coral atoll that occupies the area of about 37,000 h (91,000 acres).

Fact #6. Citizens in Solomon Islands can pay for the energy with crops

The South Pacific Applied Geoscience Commission has developed a scheme that provides the Solomon Islands citizens an access to solar energy. According to this scheme, those citizens who are unable to pay in cash can pay instead with crops.

Fact #7. Shell money is still in active use in Solomon Islands

Here goes another one of interesting facts about Solomon Islands. Despite the fact that the country has its official currency, known as Solomon Islands Dollars, shell money is still in active use here. They are most often used for ceremonial and trade purposes.

Fact #8. There are 70 languages spoken in Solomon Islands

English is the official language in Solomon Islands, but it's spoken fluently by only 1-2% of the population. The total number of spoken languages in Solomon Islands accounts for 74. Of these, 70 languages are still in active use and 4 are already extinct.

Fact #9. Solomon Islands has the most successful beach soccer team in Oceania

Another one of fun facts about Solomon Islands regards sports. Beach soccer is one of the most popular and the most played sports in the country. The Solomon Islands national team is known as the most successful beach soccer team in Oceania.

Fact #10. Poi is one of the most popular dishes in Solomon Islands

Poi is a traditional dish, made of the underground stem of taro. Traditional poi is made by mashing the stem on a wooden pounding board with a pestle. Water is added to the poi during mashing and just before eating to achieve viscous or liquid consistency.
